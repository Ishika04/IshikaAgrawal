Google Classroom

This March, schools across the country switched to remote learning as a result of the pandemic. For my younger sister, a highly motivated, straight-A high school student, the transition was a struggle. She was constantly overwhelmed with notifications, missed assignment deadlines, and found it difficult to communicate with her teachers.

Before the pandemic, teachers could easily support students in the classroom. Now, students have a lot less support than they’re used to. It’s up to them to wade through an average of 30+ Google Classroom notifications a day to find that one reminder, shoot that first email when they’re falling behind, and keep themselves organized. To solve this problem, I designed the Bulletin Board feature to allow teachers to more easily support and motivate students as schools switch to online learning.

Timeline: Aug. 2021 - Sep. 2021

Tools: Figma

Project Type: Passion project case study

Key Takeaways

Get feedback early and often

At first, I would ask for user feedback only once I felt that the designs were the absolute best I could make them. This meant that when I did get feedback, I had to make a lot of changes. Now, I try to get feedback even if I feel my work isn't yet complete.

Design is nonlinear

Oftentimes, I had to go back a couple steps when I realized that perhaps there was a different way of approaching things or that there were new constraints I had to take into account. Frequently iterating means having to go back sometimes.

Don't need to reinvent the wheel

I spent a lot of time breaking down existing design patterns, especially within the Google Classroom app in order to make sure that the UI was consistent. Even as I added new features, I realized that I could shape existing design patterns to fit the user's needs.

Understanding the User

I conducted user research interviews, selecting a diverse set of users in order to understand pain points in the Google Classroom app for both students and teachers.

The 5 User Archetypes

I found that users tended to fit into 5 main categories - the student leader, the athlete, the average student, the experimenter, and the average teacher.

Empathizing with the Target User

To design for students of diverse backgrounds and uses of Google Classroom, I created personas to highlight key needs and frustrations of users.

Understanding the Current Google Classroom App

Currently, Google Classroom tackles one part of the problem by allowing the user to manage all of their posts and notifications via the to-do list feature and the assignment stream. However, Google Classroom’s current solution lacks organization. Here are some of the pain points:

Too Much Technical Overhead

The assignment stream contains all posts from announcements to assignments, causing comments and discussion threads to get lost. This means that assignments that are due soon also get buried in the assignment stream, causing students to miss them. Thus, organization is a time consuming process.

Existing Features are Difficult to Find

There are several features that Google Classroom offers to organize assignments - the To Do List, filtering by unit, and aggregating graded/missing assignments. However, 42% of users interviewed did not know about the To Do List feature and 63% did not know about the filtering features.

Key Findings

Pinpointing the Main Problem

Students want to feel motivated in school and keep up with their coursework but it’s difficult because:

  1. There are too many posts and assignments to manage in order to stay organized
  2. There is a lack of interaction and support between students and teachers

Moving Forward with the Bulletin Board

I decided to move forward with the Bulletin Board feature because it not only helps students who are already motivated, but makes it easier for struggling students to get organized without additional effort on their part. I also decided to redesign the layout of the Classwork Tab so that it prioritizes assignments that are due soon and keeps the assignment stream solely for announcements and materials, thereby creating support.

Below is a quick overview of the new features and changes:

Bulletin Boards: Summarizing Important Information

A method for students to view brief summaries of important links and items that they need to complete for class from the home screen of the app. Advantages:

  • Requires no additional organization on the student's part
  • Quick to access and very visible
  • Reduces times spent searching for items buried in the assignment stream

Reminders: Making Information Digestable

A simple process for teachers to be able to pin certain reminders or links to their Bulletin Board.

Classwork Tab Redesign: Reducing Time Spent Searching the Stream

A method for students to view brief summaries of important links and items that they need to complete for class from the home screen of the app. Advantages:

  • Provides more content on a single assignment, reducing time spent looking up details
  • Sorts content by day/time based on user research feedback
  • Creates opportunities for the assignment stream to be a more active discussion board

How Can We Reduce Time Users Spend Searching For Assignments?

I addressed this question by redesigning the Classwork Tab and re-prioritizing it as the the first page page a user sees when they enter the class instead of the assignment stream. As I created the new design, here are some of the things I considered:

Determining What is Due Next

Older assignments get buried under newer assignments in the Assignment Stream or within large units in the Classwork Tab, causing students to miss them. I worked to simplify the process of determining what is due next by highlighting assignments due soon and adding options to hide assignments due far in the future.

Providing More Content to Reduce Time Searching for Details

Through user research, I found that the current tab does not provide enough information about each individual assignment - students have to click on every assignment individually to find out when it is due, what the assignment requires, and if anyone has responded to a comment they might have left. I aimed to reduce the number of steps by providing more content per assignment.

Simplifying the Process of Accessing Older Assignments

Google Classroom's current tools to sort through older assignments are not visible and missed by most students as evident through user research. I made these tools more visible and integrated them with a search bar to help students find things faster.

How Can We Make the Bulletin Board As Easy to Digest And Access As Possible?

Previously, there was no place for a user to view a summary of all of their classes that highlights only the most important information. Whether your looking for an assignment due right away or an important reference material, you had to search through the assignment stream. The Bulletin Board summarizes the information on the main page and the Classwork Tab. As I was created the design for it, here are some of the things that I considered:

Regulating the Amount of Information

For user research, teachers pack a lot of information into their announcements and assignments. The Bulletin Board is only effective if there's a way to regulate how many posts there are and how concise those posts are.

Sorting and Categorizing Information

There's a lot of information in the Bulletin Board, especially when you see all of them on the main page. Users need to be able to navigate this information quickly by scanning and being able to separate out what's important.

Previous Iterations

In previous iterations, I considered a variety of entry points for the Bulletin Board, different layouts that make it easier/harder to scan, dealing with different edge cases (too many posts or too many words per post), adding a new post, and what a user sees when they click on an individual reminder.

If I were to continue the project, what would I focus on next?

In the midst of the pandemic, our lives completely shifted to a virtual world and there were several problems that came with that. It was empowering to be able to use design to be able to solve a relevant problem during this time that so many students and teachers are struggling with.

If I continued the project, I would want to focus more on finding ways to better assimilate the Bulletin Board feature in Google Classroom and continue with user testing.

HMW integrate reminders into the announcement stream to function as more “urgent” announcements?

Since the Bulletin Board is meant to highlight only important information, teachers might feel limited by it's use overtime. I would want to explore how we can prioritize items within the announcement stream so that teachers can bring attention to certain announcements that might not be urgent currently but are something that they want students to keep in mind. One example of this might be pinning a class discussion about a certain chapter in the book that everyone is discussing.

HMW bring some of the elements from the new Classwork Tab into the To-Do List feature to make it easier for students to use?

Many of the design changes that I made came from feedback on the to-do list and other features that already exist within Google Classroom. I would want to explore how we can use this feedback to further iterate on these features so that they serve students better.
